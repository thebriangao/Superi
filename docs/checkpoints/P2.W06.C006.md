# P2.W06.C006

## Outcome

Implemented engine-owned A/V synchronization that turns the actual audio presentation clock and
exact video timing into explicit nonblocking hold, present, drop, correction, and discontinuity
recovery outcomes. Foreground playback now consumes that coordinator after graph, cache, and
display evaluation and before bounded viewport admission.

The interactive engine policy admits an eight millisecond presentation tolerance, starts eligible
video dropping at forty milliseconds of lateness, caps one video-only interval correction at twenty
milliseconds, limits consecutive drops to four, and treats ten seconds as an explicit timeline
discontinuity. These deterministic thresholds stay separate from the lower-level reusable scheduler
default and do not claim unmeasured physical display or audio latency.

## Architecture and integration

`AvSyncCoordinator` composes the concurrency-owned `PlaybackClock` and `AvSyncScheduler` rather than
duplicating either system. It observes master time only on the playback execution domain, passes
immutable frame PTS and duration plus caller-owned readiness into the scheduler, and converts the
result into an engine outcome carrying exact drift, master time, display interval, correction,
presentation reason, and recovery evidence.

A scheduler rebase request is applied immediately by the concrete clock owner. The target is
rescaled exactly into the clock timebase, the active audio or monotonic mapping is reanchored once,
and the same frame is scheduled again against the recovered master. The resulting presentation
retains both the original discontinuity and the aligned final drift, so recovery is observable and
does not become a silent clock jump.

`PlaybackOrchestrator` now owns the coordinator instead of a bare clock. Its existing constructor
uses the interactive policy, and an explicit-policy constructor supports deterministic integration
tests and future engine configuration. `PlaybackPoll` carries the complete synchronization outcome
for waiting, presentation, viewport backpressure, and dropping, while policy and statistics are
available as coherent snapshots.

The current foreground owner has one evaluated or retained frame, so it reports that frame as both
nondroppable and without a ready successor. Late single-frame playback therefore shortens the live
display interval within policy and remains visible rather than inventing successor readiness. The
public coordinator accepts explicit successor-ready and drop-eligible facts for the adjacent
transport queue, and its contract proves that both facts are required before a late drop occurs.

## Normal, stressed, and recovery behavior

On-time video presents at its nominal duration. Early video returns a bounded hold interval without
sleeping or blocking the playback domain. Moderately late video retains its exact media duration
but publishes a separately shortened live display interval. Frames beyond the drop threshold remain
visible when protected or when no successor is ready, while explicitly eligible frames with a ready
successor can be dropped under the existing starvation guard.

Viewport saturation retains both the exact owned frame and its already resolved presentation. A
later retry therefore cannot apply the same rebase twice, repeat a correction decision, or inflate
scheduler presentation statistics. Graph, color, alpha, audio-capacity, and worker failures retain
their existing classified behavior and do not mutate the synchronization owner.

A large timestamp discontinuity reanchors once and presents the same exact frame in the same
nonblocking poll. Audio device loss still falls back to monotonic playback at the current timeline
position, and restoration still binds the actual device clock at the then-current position. Later
audio samples continue from that shared timeline without integrating rounded polling deltas.

## Rendering and export coherence

Live synchronization wraps `AvSyncFrameTiming` around the graph result's exact PTS and duration and
returns those values unchanged in every outcome. A shortened live display interval is distinct from
the nominal media duration, and a rebase changes only the active playback clock mapping. Deterministic
rendering and export can therefore consume the same exact timeline and frame timing without inheriting
wall-clock waits, late-frame drops, or live display corrections.

This checkpoint does not create a second render evaluator, change decoded or color metadata, alter
audio samples, or move offline export onto a device clock. The separate render and export owner may
compose the same exact graph artifacts while remaining sample-accurate and independent of real-time
quality-of-service decisions.

## Research basis

The official GStreamer synchronization design was the primary reference for one pipeline clock,
audio-device sample counters as a master source, monotonically comparable running time, and frame
presentation against that clock:
<https://gstreamer.freedesktop.org/documentation/additional/design/synchronisation.html>.

The official GStreamer quality-of-service design informed explicit signed lateness, observable
drop and correction evidence, and the preference for reducing video work instead of dropping audio:
<https://gstreamer.freedesktop.org/documentation/additional/design/qos.html>. Its typical threshold
is descriptive rather than normative for Superi because the ratified product target is stricter.

The official GStreamer latency design informed finite buffering, nonblocking sink ownership, and
explicit base-time redistribution after resynchronization:
<https://gstreamer.freedesktop.org/documentation/additional/design/latency.html>. The implementation
uses the repository's existing permissively licensed dependencies and adds no network or closed-tier
path.

## Verification

The engine coordination contract was written first and failed only because the placeholder exported
none of the planned types. Its five deterministic cases prove playback-domain ownership, the eight
millisecond policy, early hold, on-time presentation, bounded late correction, protected behavior,
eligible dropping, exact discontinuity recovery, coherent statistics, and continuous audio loss and
restoration.

The existing playback integration contract was strengthened before integration and failed because
poll results and orchestrator diagnostics had no synchronization evidence. Its real graph, cache,
color, audio, clock, worker, and viewport path now proves early waiting, on-time presentation,
backpressure without duplicate observations, thirty-millisecond lateness correction, a greater-than
ten-second discontinuity recovery, unchanged frame PTS and duration, degraded graph and alpha
failure, bounded audio saturation, and continuous clock fallback and restoration.

Focused contracts, the complete engine package, strict all-target engine Clippy, concurrency A/V
scheduler contracts, audio timeline synchronization, device-output contracts including the
long-session drift case, map validation, and the repository final verifier form the local completion
evidence. Exact commands and results remain in the ignored checkpoint execution log.

## Remaining constraints

The foreground playback owner still evaluates one frame at a time, so actual queue-based successor
selection and semantic drop eligibility belong to the adjacent transport checkpoint. Source session
preparation, native GPU surface presentation, physical device latency, display timing, constrained
hardware performance, and soak evidence remain with their documented owners and hardware lanes.

The eight millisecond tolerance is a scheduling contract, not proof that a specific physical system
keeps observed A/V offset below ten milliseconds. Fresh physical `audio`, `performance`, and `soak`
lane evidence remains required before a release can make that hardware claim.
